Photo by @estherhorvath | Some 400,000 specimens from German citizens, collected over four decades and stored in an old military bunker,  trace the rise—and sometimes the fall—of chemical pollutants in an industrial country. 

At the German Environmental Specimen Bank, Dominik Lermen stands among cryo-storage tanks that hold thousands of vials of blood and urine. Lerman leads the team that collects samples from volunteers every year and stores them here on behalf of the German Environment Agency. 

It's part of a federal ministry's effort to “to systematically monitor and analyze human exposure to chemicals like lead, mercury, plasticizers, and others,” Lermen says. The project traces which substances have  accumulated at potentially dangerous levels, and considers whether bans and regulations of some of those substances have been effective.
